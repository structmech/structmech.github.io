EXERCISES IN EUCLIDEAN SPACE AND LINEAR MAPPINGS

Exercise 1: Verify that given \((a_1, a_2, …, a_n)\in\mathbb{R}^n\), the set of all \((x_1, x_2, ..., x_n)\in \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2+ … + a_nx_n=0\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Solution: Let \(S\) be the set of all \((x_1, x_2, ..., x_n)\in \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2+ … + a_nx_n=0\). For any \(\mathbf{v}\in S\), \(\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n\) therefore \(S\subset \mathbb{R}^n\).

Let \(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}\) be any two vectors in \(S\). Then \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w} = (v_1+w_1, v_2+w_2, …, v_n+w_n)\). It follows that \begin{equation*}(a_1(v_1+w_1) +a_2(v_2+w_2)+ … +a_n(v_n+w_n)) =(a_1v_1+a_2v_2+ … +a_nv_n+a_1w_1+a_2w_2+…+a_nw_n)\end{equation*} Because \(\mathbf{v}\in S, a_1v_1+a_2v_2+…+a_nv_n=0\) and because \(\mathbf{w}\in S, a_1w_1+a_2w_2+…+a_nw_n=0\). Therefore, \( (a_1(v_1+w_1) + a_2 (v_2+w_2) + … +a_n(v_n+w_n)) = 0 \quad \) which means that \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\in S\). Therefore, \(S\) is closed under the operation of vector addition.

Let \(\alpha \in\mathbb{R}\) be any real number. Then, \(\alpha\mathbf{v}=(\alpha v_1, \alpha v_2, …, \alpha v_n)\) and \(a_1\alpha v_1 +a_2 \alpha v_2 + … +a_n \alpha v_n =\alpha(a_1v_1+a_2v_2+…+a_nv_n)=0\). This shows that \(\alpha \mathbf{v}\in S\) and \(S\) is closed under the operation of multiplication by a scalar.

Because \(S\) is a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and it is closed under the operations of vector addition and multiplication by a scalar, we conclude that \(S\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Exercise 2: Prove that the intersection of two subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is also a subspace.

Solution: Let \(S_1,S_2\) be subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and let \(S=S_1\cap S_2\). For any \(\mathbf{v}\in S\), \(\mathbf{v}\in \mathbb{R}^n\), therefore \(S\) is a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

For any \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in S\), \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in S_1\) and \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in S_2\). Since \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) are both subspaces, \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\in S_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\in S_2\) which means that \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\in S\). Therefore, \(S\) is closed under the operation of vector addition.

Let \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\) be any real number. Because \(\mathbf{v}\in S_1\), \(\mathbf{v}\in S_2\) and \(S_1,S_2\) are closed under the operation of multiplication by a scalar, \(\alpha v\in S_1\) and \(\alpha v\in S_2\). It follows that \(\alpha v\in S\) and \(S\) is closed under the operation of multiplication by a scalar.

We conclude that since \(S\) is a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(S\) is closed under the operations of vector addition and multiplication by a scalar, \(S\) is a subspace.

Exercise 4: If \(V\) is the set of all \((x,y,z)\in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \(x+2y=0\), and \(x+y=3z\), show that \(V\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Solution: \(V\) is clearly a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Let \(\overrightarrow{x_0}=(x_0,y_0,z_0),\overrightarrow{x_1}=(x_1,y_1,z_1)\in V\). Then \(\overrightarrow{x_0}+\overrightarrow{x_1}=(x_0+x_1,y_0+y_1,z_0+z_1)\) and \(x_0+2y_0=0\), \(x_1+2y_1=0\), \(x_0+y_0=3z_0\) and \(x_1+y_1=3z_1\).

It follows that \((x_0+x_1)+2(y_0+y_1)=(x_0+2y_0)+(x_1+2y_1)=0\).

Also, \((x_0+x_1)+(y_0+y_1)=(x_0+y_0)+(x_1+y_1)=3z_0+3z_1=3(z_0+z_1)\). Therefore, \(\overrightarrow{x_0}+\overrightarrow{x_1}\in V\).

Let \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then \(\alpha\overrightarrow{x_0}=(\alpha x_0, \alpha y_0, \alpha z_0)\) and \(\alpha x_0+2\alpha y_0=\alpha(x_0+2y_0)=0\).

Also, \(\alpha x_0+\alpha y_0=\alpha(x_0+y_0)=\alpha(3 z_0)=3\alpha z_0\). Therefore, \(\alpha\overrightarrow{x_0}\in V\). Since \(V\) is a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and \(V\) is closed under the operations of vector addition and multiplication by a scalar, \(V\) is a subspace.

Exercise 5: Let \(D_0\) be the set of all differentiable real-valued functions on \([0,1]\) such that \(f(0)=f(1)=0\). If \(f+g\) and \(\alpha f\) are defined by \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\) and \((\alpha f)(x)=\alpha f(x)\) show that \(D_0\) is a vector space. Would \(D_0\) still be a vetor space if the condition \(f(0)=f(1)=0\) is replaced by \(f(0)=0\) and \(f(1)=1\)?

Solution: Let \(f,g\in D_0\). Then \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\in\mathbb{R}\) and \((f+g)^{\prime}(x)=f^{\prime}(x)+g^{\prime}(x)\) \(\forall x\in[0,1]\). Clearly, \(f+g\) is a differentiable real-valued function on \([0,1]\). Also, \((f+g)(0)=f(0)+g(0)=0+0=0\) and \((f+g)(1)=f(1)+g(1)=0+0=0\) which means that \(f+g\in D_0\).

Let \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\). Then \((\alpha f)(x)=\alpha f(x)\in \mathbb{R}\) and \((\alpha f)^{\prime}(x)=\alpha f^{\prime}(x)\) \(\forall x\in[0,1]\). Clearly, \((\alpha f)\) is a differentiable real-valued function on \([0,1]\) . Also, \((\alpha f)(0)=\alpha f(0)=\alpha 0=0\) and \((\alpha f)(1)=\alpha f(1)=\alpha 0=0\) which means that \((\alpha f)\in D_0\).

On the other hand, if \(f(0)=0,\text{ }f(1)=1\text{ }\forall f\in D_0\) and \(f,g\in D_0\), then \((f+g)(1)=f(1)+g(1)=1+1=2\) which means that \(f+g\not\in D_0\), \(D_0\) is not closed under vector addition and \(D_0\) is not a vector space.

Exercise 6: Given a set \(S\), denote by \(\mathcal{F}(S,\mathbb{R})\) the set of all real-valued functions from \(S\) to \(\mathbb{R}\). Show that \(\mathcal{F}(S,\mathbb{R})\) is a vector space with \(f+g\) and \(\alpha f\) defined as in Exercise 5.

Solution:Let \(f,g\in\mathcal{F}\). For any \(x\in S,\text{ }\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\in\mathbb{R},\text{ }(\alpha f)(x)=\alpha f(x)\in\mathbb{R}\). Therefore, \(f+g\in\mathcal{F},\text{ }\alpha f\in\mathcal{F}\). Since \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under vector addition and multiplication by a scalar, it is a vector space.
