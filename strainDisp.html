STRAIN DISPLACEMENT RELATIONSHIPS

The displacements of a point \((x,y,z)\) in a three dimensional body in \(x,y,z\) directions are denoted with the letters \(u,v,w\) respectively. Displacement is a function of position, so we can write \(u(x,y,z),v(x,y,z),w(x,y,z)\). We distinguish between normal strains due to the elongation or shortening of an element inside the body and the shearing strains due to the distortion of that same element. The normal strains are denoted with \(\epsilon_{xx},\epsilon_{yy},\epsilon_{zz}\) and the shearing strains are denoted with \(\epsilon_{xy},\epsilon_{yz},\epsilon_{xz}\). Fig. 1 shows the displacement of an infinitesimal part of a body from the initial position 0 to the deformed position 1.

Using the definition of strain, \(\epsilon_{xx}\) can be calculated as in Eq.\eqref{eq:epsxx} where \(L_{x0}\) and \(L_{x1}\) denote the lengths in x-direction of the elements 0 and 1 respectively. \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xx}=\lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{[L_{x1}]-[L_{x0}]}{L_{x0}}\lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{[x+\Delta x+u(x+\Delta x,y,z)-(x+u(x,y,z))]-[\Delta x]}{\Delta x}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \label{eq:epsxx} \end{equation}

Similarly, we can also obtain \(\epsilon_{yy}=\displaystyle\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\) and \(\epsilon_{zz}=\displaystyle\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\)

The blue rectangular shape in Fig. 2 belongs to the initial configuration of a body on the x-y plane before shearing deformation. After the deformation, this rectangle is distorted by the angles \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). The shearing strain in the x-y plane is defined as in Eq.\eqref{eq:epsxy} \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xy}=\epsilon_{yx}=\frac{1}{2}(\alpha +\beta) \label{eq:epsxy} \end{equation}

In the linear elastic range the angles of distortion are small and we can assume \(\alpha \approxeq \tan\alpha \), \(\beta\approxeq\tan\beta\) without introducing any considerable errors into the calculation. Furthermore, Eq.\eqref{eq:partialShear} holds true. \begin{equation} \begin{split} \alpha\Delta x=\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\Delta x\Rightarrow\boxed{\alpha=\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}},\quad \beta\Delta y=\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\Delta y\Rightarrow\boxed{\beta=\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}}\Rightarrow \epsilon_{xy}=\frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\Big) \end{split} \label{eq:partialShear} \end{equation} Similarly it can be shown that \(\epsilon_{yz}=\epsilon_{zy}=\frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y} \Big)\) and \(\epsilon_{xz}=\epsilon_{zx}=\frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\Big)\). These results can be summarized in a strain matrix (or tensor) as shown in Eq.\eqref{eq:strmat} \begin{equation} \epsilon=\begin{bmatrix} \epsilon_{xx} & \epsilon_{xy} & \epsilon_{xz} \\ \epsilon_{yx} & \epsilon_{yy} & \epsilon_{yz} \\ \epsilon_{zx} & \epsilon_{zy} & \epsilon_{zz} \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\Big) & \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\Big) \\ \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\Big) & \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} & \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y} \Big) \\ \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\Big) & \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y} \Big) & \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \end{bmatrix} \label{eq:strmat} \end{equation}
